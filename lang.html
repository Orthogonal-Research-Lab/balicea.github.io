Books, Articles, and Other Mediums.

This page provides information on the various languages spoken throughout Amazônia.

1. Books

Basso, Ellen B. ed.
1977 Carib-Speaking Indians: Culture, Society, and Language. University of Arizona Press, Tucson.

This is a survey of the many language groups in the Carib family. Included in this volume is a chart with the subdivisions of Carib, sociocultural information about Carib-speakers, and maps of how the various languages diffused across South America and the Carribean.

Jackson, Jean E.
1983 The Fish People: Linguistic Exogamy and Tukanoan Identity in Northwest Amazônia. Cambridge University Press, Cambridge, UK.

Jean Jackson takes a social anthropological view of how language affects social relations and cultural identity. She also covers Tukanoan economic relations, settlement patterns, and cosmology in this work.

3. Films, Web Sites, Etc.

Ethnologue (http://www. sil.org/ethnologue/)

This site, edited by Barbara F. Grimes, is a project of the Summer Institute of Linguistics in Dallas. Under the Americas heading, all of the languages spoken in Amazonia are listed according to their home nation-state. Links are provided to maps, language family trees, and other useful resources.

Instituto Socioambiental (http://www.socioambiental.org/)

A Portuguese-language site dedicated to news on local populations, language and census information, and discussing current events in Amazônia. Instituto Socioambiental exists "to preserve indigenous diversity in the face of globalization".

Interactive Guarani Dictionary (http://www.eskimo.com/~krautml)

A page that offers translations of words from Spanish and German into Guarani, a Tupi-Guarani language indigenous to Paraguay. Tupi-Guarani is a language group spoken throughout Amazônia.

Taino Language Project (http://members.dandy.net/~orocobix/ tedict.html)

This site gives an A to Z listing of Taino phrases and their English translation. It also talks about the history of the Taino people in Puerto Rico, who have roots in the tropical lowlands of South America. In addition, the Taino language is part of the Tupi-Guarani family, which is prevalent in parts of Amazônia.
