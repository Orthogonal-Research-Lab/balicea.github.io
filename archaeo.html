Books, Articles, and Other Mediums.

Here is an exploration and summation of various archaeological projects ongoing in Amazonia, exploring both the contemporary frontier and prehistoric lifeways.

1. Books

Roosevelt, Anna Curtenius
1980 Parmaná: Prehistoric Maize and Manioc Subsistence Along the Amazon and Orinoco. Academic Press, New York.

Roosevelt chronicles her own research to challenge the conventional view that Amazônia has traditionally been exclusively home to forest subsistence strategies. She introduces theories by Meggers, Carneiro, and others, and then tells how the data from Parmaná better supports an alternative view of prehistoric subsistence.

Willey, Gordon R.
1971 An Introduction to American Archaeology Volume Two: South America. Prentice-Hall, Englewood Cliffs, NJ.

Gordon Willey offers information about the culture history of Amazônia in Chapter 6. He discusses all aspects of the prehistoric and contemporary indigenous people of the region, and also offers the reader maps, pictures, and a chronological chart of stylistic phases of pottery design.

2. Articles

Meggers, Betty
1951 Pre-Columbian Colonization of the Amazon. Archaeology 4:110-114.

In this article, Meggers investigates several earthen mounds on Marajó Island at the mouth of the Amazon. She uses pottery styles to examine the apex and eventual demise of the Marajoara culture. Meggers also speculates about the social structure and cultural history of these people.

Roosevelt, A.C., M. Lima da Costa, C. Lopes Machado, M. Michab H. Vallados, N. Toth, J. Feathers, M. Imazio da Silveira, B. Chernoff, N. Mercier, J.A. Holman, K. Schick, D.S. Reese, W. Barnett
1996 Paleoindian Cave Dwellers in the Amazon: The Peopling of the Americas. Science 19 April: 373-384.

This 1996 article is significant because it presents the notion of habitation at Monte Alegre in Brasil during the late Pleistocene. Previously unthinkable, the notion of non-Clovis cultures in the Americas during this time period is advocated by Roosevelt and others.

Wilbert, Johannes
1977 Navigators of the Winter Sun. In The Sea in the Pre-Columbian World. Elizabeth P. Benson ed. Dunbarton Oaks, Washington D.C.

Wilbert discusses the cultural history of the canoe in Warao society. Even though the Warao reside in the Orinoco region, this article is relevant to experimental archaeology projects in Amazônia dedicated to building a typical river canoe. Navigators also contains sources on how canoes are important to indigenous cosmology and mythology. The author also demonstrates the stages involved in building a canoe and the technologies used.

3. Films, Web Sites, Etc.

C 14 Decomposition Calculator. (http://artalpha.anu.edu.au/web/arc/resources/papers/ausdates/ ausdates.htm")

An Australian site that calculates how much Carbon 14 is left in an artifact or faunal remain after so many years, this link is also relevant to dating remains from research being done in Amazonia.

Dendrochronology/ Tree-Ring Resources. (http://dizzy.library. edu/users/mount/treering.html)

This is a site sponsored by the University of Arizona library that offers links to information on dendrochronology, GIS, and hydrology. Dendrochronology is a dating method performed by measuring and counting the rings of living and dead trees and then determining the age of associated artifacts. Such a method is only good for dating finds younger than 5000 years old.

Sheffield Dendrochronology Lab. (http://www.shef.ac.uk/uni/ academic/A-C/ap/dendro.html)

A British site that gives general and contact information on the Dendrochronology Lab at the University of Sheffield. The site also contains a summation of the lab's current research projects.
